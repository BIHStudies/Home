The Riddle of Instinct & The Origin of Life

A Collection of BIHS Research

Ethology, the scientific study of animal behavior, categorizes actions as inherited (instinctive), learned, or a combination. Inherited behaviors are innate; individuals instinctively know how to find food, avoid predators, and reproduce. Learned behaviors are acquired through experience. The third category combines these, like birds born with a basic song pattern they refine by listening to their parents.

Our focus is on the miraculous realm of instinctive behaviors. While science meticulously describes *how* these instincts manifest, the philosophical question of *where they come from* remains largely unanswered by conventional explanation.

The Malleefowl: A Marvel of Instinct

A prime example is the Australian Malleefowl, a bird renowned for its extraordinary breeding method. Unlike most birds, the malleefowl employs an external heating system: an elaborate incubation mound.

First, a male and female pair excavate a five-meter-diameter hole. They fill it with leaves and twigs, awaiting rain to saturate the organic material. Once wet, the vegetation is covered with sandy soil. The female then lays one egg per week into the mound for nine months.

The true marvel is the male's role. For the entire nine-month period, he diligently manages the mound's temperature. With an astonishing heat-sensing ability in his beak, he can detect deviations and adjust the mound's heat to within one degree Celsius of the ideal 34°C. If it's too hot, he removes soil; if too cold, he adds more sand. This is a constant effort, moving an average of 85 kg of soil daily.

When the chicks hatch, they face another instinctive challenge: digging themselves out. They emerge fully developed and immediately abandon the mound to fend for themselves, having never learned how to build such a structure. Their knowledge is entirely inborn.

Perspectives on Purpose

This complex "instinctive chain"—involving the female, the male's nine-month temperature regulation, and the chick's escape—presents a profound logical challenge to gradual evolutionary development. All elements must be present simultaneously for the species to reproduce. If any part were missing, the species would cease to exist.

Mainstream biology attributes such features to long-term evolution. However, these explanations often remain general, lacking viable, step-by-step pathways for such interdependent systems to evolve. Contemporary AI, accessing vast scientific literature, acknowledges that the malleefowl's behavior "lacks a clear empirical, logical, and existential explanation within the framework of evolution."

The dialogue between science and Bhagavata wisdom, therefore, does not ask us to abandon reason, but to recognize its boundaries. By embracing a 'trans-rational' framework where contradictions resolve, the pursuit of knowledge transforms from an act of intellectual conquest into an art of humble reception. We are left to ponder: if reality itself operates beyond binary logic, what must we cultivate within our own consciousness to perceive it more fully?
