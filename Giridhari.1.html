The Physicalism Fallacy:

How Modern Science Rediscovered Meaning

Fine-Tuning, Cosmic Codes, and the Search for Purpose It’s a funny thing, isn't it? Modern science is more powerful than ever, capable of peering into the farthest reaches of the cosmos and the tiniest building blocks of life. Yet, for all our empirical prowess, so many of us feel adrift—disoriented, anxious, and grappling with a profound sense of existential emptiness. Why is that? I think a big part of the problem comes from an unintended side effect of our scientific progress: a philosophy, or maybe even a kind of religion, called physicalism. Physicalism is the belief that the only things that are real or meaningful are the things we can touch, measure, and quantify. If it’s not physical, it doesn’t count. This worldview often leads to the conclusion that we live in a random universe, a cosmic accident with no inherent meaning. The irony is, this happens when we forget what science is truly about. I can tell you this from personal experience. More than thirty years ago, I was a staunch atheist. I didn’t believe in anything that wasn’t “scientific” as I understood it. But then, a series of personal events left me feeling disoriented and uneasy. I started asking myself the big questions. Is there any meaning to all this? Am I just a random collection of atoms, making decisions without any real purpose? It was a question that began to haunt me, one that I think haunts many of us: “What if the universe has no purpose?” At the time, I was already a scientist, deeply fascinated by mathematics and astronomy. I’d always dismissed religious texts like the Bible as mere mythology. But then a little voice of true scientific inquiry piped up inside me. “Okay,” it said, “maybe that’s true, but you haven’t even read it. How can you judge something you know nothing about?” And so, my journey began. It started with the simple scientific principle of being objective enough to examine the evidence.

Whispers of Design in the Cosmos

If we’re going to look for hints of intention in the universe, the best place to start is with science itself. And the first thing science tells us is that we know we don’t know! It’s a humbling reality for cosmologists that only about 5% of the cosmos is made of the ordinary, detectable matter we’re familiar with. The rest is a mystery. About 27% is dark matter, and a whopping 68% is dark energy. Think about that. The vast majority of our reality is made of stuff we can’t see and don’t understand. These “dark” components are shaping everything, orchestrating the cosmic dance from behind a curtain. Our universe is, in a very real sense, utterly unseen. This is the framework we’re working in. But even within the 5% we “can” see, the clues are staggering. The late astrophysicist Sir Martin Rees wrote a brilliant book called “Just Six Numbers”, which highlights how a handful of fundamental constants in physics appear to be exquisitely fine-tuned to allow for a universe that can support life. Imagine you’re in the cockpit of a fantastically complex airplane. In front of you is a dashboard with countless dials, each one controlling a fundamental aspect of the aircraft. For the plane to fly safely from one point to another, every single dial has to be set to an exquisitely precise value. Nudge one just a fraction of a millimeter, and the whole thing might fall out of the sky. Our universe, it turns out, is a bit like that cockpit. If gravity were just a tiny bit stronger relative to electromagnetism, stars would burn out too quickly for life to evolve. A little weaker, and stars and galaxies might never have formed at all. Another dial, which scientists call epsilon (ε), controls the efficiency of nuclear fusion in stars. If this value were slightly different, stars wouldn’t be able to forge the elements necessary for life, like carbon and oxygen. We are, quite literally, children of the stars; the atoms in your body were cooked up inside a stellar furnace. This process only works because that dial is set “just right”. And the list goes on… When you see all these dials lined up so perfectly, it’s hard not to wonder. The next step is a philosophical one, but it’s an intuitive leap: if something is so perfectly arranged, doesn’t that suggest someone arranged it? When we see an intricate house, we assume there was an architect. This is the argument from design, and while it’s not strictly science, it’s a powerful conclusion that the science seems to be pointing toward.

Beyond Space and Time: Are We in a Cosmic Video Game?

The hints of design don’t stop with cosmology. You can find similar arguments for fine-tuning and improbable events in biology, genetics, and neuroscience. But modern physics is pushing us toward an even more radical idea: what if our reality isn’t as real as we think? Physicists are currently struggling to unite our two best theories of the universe: Einstein’s theory of general relativity (which describes gravity on a large scale) and quantum mechanics (which describes the bizarre world of subatomic particles). The two are spectacularly successful in their own domains, but they are fundamentally incompatible. This has led some scientists to propose that space and time themselves might not be fundamental. Instead, they could be emergent properties, like the pixels on a screen that arise from a deeper, underlying code. This might sound like something straight out of a sci-fi movie, but what if we're all players in a cosmic video game? I know, it's a wild idea, but the "simulation hypothesis" is being taken seriously by many scientists and philosophers. It suggests there’s a layer of pure information that is more fundamental than the physical world we experience. Our universe, with its rules and landscapes, is rendered from this code, much like a computer renders a virtual world for you to explore. This is where Bhāgavata cosmology offers a strikingly similar perspective. It describes our material world as Māyā, often translated as “illusion”. This doesn’t mean the world isn't real, any more than a video game isn't real to the person playing it. It’s a designed experience with real interactions and real consequences, but our roles within it are temporary. The Bhāgavata philosophy goes even deeper, describing a living informational structure called the Virāṭ Rūpa. This structure contains all the relational information of the cosmos—our karma, our destinies, our desires—from the moment of creation. Māyā is like the cosmic graphics card that renders this information into the tangible, experiential reality we navigate. And crucially, this structure is responsive to consciousness. We aren’t passive avatars in a pre-programmed simulation; we are active players with free will. Our choices act as a cosmic feedback system, shaping our experience of the game.

Getting Lost in the Game

You might wonder, “If this is just a game, why does it feel so real? Why are we so easily affected by it?” Well, think about how easily we get lost in experiences we know are fabricated. A friend of mine who teaches middle school did an experiment with VR headsets. He had his students, one by one, put on a headset that simulated walking across a narrow plank over a deep canyon. He told me that the moment they put the headsets on, they were overwhelmed with anxiety. They’d cry out, “I’m falling! I’m scared!” What's fascinating is that their classmates, standing right beside them in the safety of the classroom, would be shouting, “It’s not real! You’re just wearing a headset!” But it didn’t matter. The emotional reality of the experience completely overrode their intellectual knowledge that it was fake. This is a perfect illustration of what Bhāgavata philosophy calls ahaṅkāra, or the false ego. It’s the deep-seated conditioning that makes us believe we are the player in the game—that our body, our personality, our social roles are our true self. Like a gamer completely lost in their avatar, we forget that our true identity is the consciousness experiencing the game, the *ātman*. We become so attached to the successes and failures of our character that we are tossed about by the emotions of the game.

A Call for Integrated Inquiry

So, what does this all have to do with science? We started with cosmology and ended up discussing cosmic video games and the nature of the self. It seems we’ve drifted far from the laboratory, but I believe this is exactly where we need to go. We need to expand our model of inquiry. The Bhāgavata tradition outlines three primary ways of knowing: pratyakṣa (direct perception and measurement—the domain of science), anumāna (logic and inference), and śabda (knowledge received from an authoritative source, or revealed truth). Science is brilliant at the first two, but it was never meant to explain everything. To get a complete picture, we need to integrate it with philosophy and metaphysics. Science studies the rules of the game; metaphysics explores the code behind it. This is a call for an integrated inquiry. We don't need blind faith, but we do need a science that acknowledges consciousness as fundamental, not just an accidental byproduct of matter. We need a spirituality that is open to rational investigation. Most importantly, we need to recognize that the philosophy we bring to our scientific work shapes the conclusions we draw. A scientist who believes everything is random will interpret data differently than one who is open to the possibility of design. We need scientists who are aware of their philosophy and philosophers who are aware of our science. Ultimately, this understanding isn’t about escaping the world. It’s about living in it with purpose. When we start to see the universe not as a random accident but as a conscious design, a grand experience crafted for our evolution, everything changes. The anxiety and disorientation of a meaningless existence begin to fade, replaced by a sense of wonder and participation.
